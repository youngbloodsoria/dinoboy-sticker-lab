Hi everyone, I’m Alex’s sister, Hayley, and he handed me the keyboard for this update. I recently returned home after spending two weeks with the family, and I wanted to share a little of what I saw and felt while I was there.

It’s hard to fully grasp what a family goes through with a cancer diagnosis. Imagine sand, thick and heavy, rising around you until you can barely move. Four years ago, when they first got the diagnosis, it was a foot of sand. A horrible thing to hear, but there was still hope and space to breathe. Slowly, year by year, the sand kept rising. Up to their waists, then their chests. Alex, Paula, Brighton, and Izzie all learning to keep moving through life with that weight pressing in around them.

When I arrived, the sand felt like it had filled the entire house. Blinding. Suffocating. Heavy enough to make my eyes water the moment I walked through the door. And yet, I was amazed at how well they had learned to keep moving through something that nearly knocked me over immediately.

The first things I noticed were the piles of Legos covering the dining room table, entire armies built with unopened boxes stacked nearby, a neat pile of hospital bills on the kitchen counter, and a large bed sitting in the middle of the living room with baskets of supplies underneath and a wheelchair beside it.

Such strange things to see in the center of a home.

But to them, it is normal now.

Working a full-time job while running your own company is hard enough, then add your sick, sleeping son in the middle of the house.

Going to high school, taking AP classes, and recovering from a soccer injury is hard enough, then add your brother unable to move himself a few feet away in the living room.

Running a business, making dinner, cleaning the house, and managing a family is hard enough, then add being a nurse at home for your son who is in pain.

The bills still come in. The groceries still need buying. The dishes still need washing.

Life keeps moving whether you’re ready or not.

I quickly found my own small routine while I was there. Filling juice cups, walking the dogs, organizing cupboards while everyone buzzed around me. I moved Brighton’s feet every hour or so, turned on calming music, watched movies with him.

And then, sweet Brighton, who doesn’t want to cause too much trouble, loves chatting about philosophy and World War II, advocates for himself so bravely in hospital rooms, and randomly breaks into singing “Man or Muppet” from The Muppets. One of the movies we watched together that he loved was The Devil Wears Prada.

When he needed to be moved and the fear crept in, we practiced deep expanding belly breaths together, filling up his diaphragm like a balloon until the panic settled. After a few days, Brighton trusted me to help move his fragile body.

But mostly, he slept.

The days were long and quiet and heavy.

The nights were my favorite.

That’s when Brighton was awake and talking. When everyone gathered together on the couch. We watched movies, played trivia, laughed, and talked about everything and nothing all at once.

Somewhere in those two weeks, without even realizing it, I learned how to move through the sand too.

There is nothing correct to say to someone carrying the weight this family carries. No amount of help that fully lifts that burden.

But I am so grateful I got to be there.

And even more grateful they let me in.
