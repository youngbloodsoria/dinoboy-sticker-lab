I’ve been thinking a lot about faith lately.

Not because I’ve suddenly found it.

Because people keep bringing it to my front door.

I grew up Mormon.

I served a mission in Taiwan. I worked hard. I followed the rules. I believed deeply. I came home, married Paula in the San Diego Temple, started a family, and tried to build a good life.

Years later, my questions became bigger than my certainty.

Some of the answers I was given didn’t make sense anymore.

So I stepped away.

That journey deserves its own story someday.

This one is about suffering.

One thing I’ve learned through Brighton’s illness is that people desperately want pain to make sense.

They want an explanation.

A formula.

A reason.

Because the alternative is terrifying.

The alternative is admitting that sometimes terrible things happen and nobody gets to tie it up neatly with a bow.

Over the years we’ve heard everything.

“Everything happens for a reason.”

“God doesn’t make mistakes.”

“If you have enough faith.”

“God has a plan.”

And I know most of those comments come from a place of love.

But sometimes love is wrong.

Sometimes people say things because they make themselves feel better, not because they help the family living through it.

The one that gets me the most is:

“God doesn’t make mistakes.”

Really?

Go spend a day on the oncology floor at CHOC.

Or Rady Children’s.

Watch children missing limbs.

Watch children recovering from surgeries.

Watch children who can’t walk.

Watch parents sleeping in chairs.

Watch nurses become family because mom and dad have to work.

Watch a fourteen-year-old boy struggle through pain that most adults couldn’t tolerate.

Then tell me again that none of this is a mistake.

Because from where I’m sitting, if this is all part of some perfect plan, then I have some questions.

The other one that makes my blood boil is:

“If you had more faith…”

Let’s stop right there.

What exactly are you saying?

That families whose children survive believed harder?

That families whose children die didn’t?

That somehow the outcome is tied to the quality of someone’s belief?

That is an unbelievably cruel thing to place on a parent, whether you mean it that way or not.

The reality is we’ve looked at everything.

We’ve talked to experts.

We’ve chased treatments.

We’ve researched.

We’ve asked questions.

We’ve fought.

We don’t need medical advice from TikTok.

We don’t need miracle cures forwarded from Facebook.

And we definitely don’t need somebody explaining our own experience back to us.

What we need is much simpler.

Bring food.

Mow the lawn.

Walk the dog.

Take the trash out.

Sit with us.

Tell a joke.

Give a hug.

Or just show up and say nothing at all.

The people who have helped us the most were never the people with answers.

They were the people willing to carry a little bit of the weight.

After four years of this, I’ve become deeply suspicious of certainty.

But I have tremendous faith in kindness.

I’ve seen what that can do.
